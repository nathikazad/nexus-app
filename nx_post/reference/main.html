The Attention Economy and its Discontents

Oct 24, 2023
6 min read
Economics Personal

In an age where information is abundant, attention becomes the scarce resource. We explore how modern systems are designed to harvest this resource and the subtle ways it shifts our cognitive priorities away from deep work.

The transition from an industrial economy to an information economy didn't just change what we produce; it fundamentally altered what is extracted. It is no longer coal or oil, but human focus. When a product is free, as the old adage goes, you are the product. But more accurately, your immediate, unfragmented attention is the product.

The Architecture of Distraction

Consider the physical spaces we build for productivity versus the digital spaces. A well-designed office or library limits peripheral distraction. It uses walls, muted acoustics, and structured layouts to channel focus.

Digital interfaces, however, are architected precisely for the opposite. They employ intermittent variable rewards—a concept borrowed directly from slot machine design. The little red notification dot is a masterclass in psychological manipulation. It offers just enough promise of social connection or urgent information to break concentration.

"We are substituting the difficult and rewarding work of meaning-making for the easy and shallow work of data consumption."

Reclaiming this attention isn't simply a matter of turning off notifications. It requires a deliberate restructuring of how we value our own cognitive time. It means treating a two-hour block of uninterrupted thought as an asset to be guarded, rather than a gap to be filled.
